Korean Food Guide

A culinary journey exploring the distinct flavors, colors, and interactive dining rituals of traditional Korean cooking.

Korea's Legendary Flavors

Korean cuisine is centered on balance, fermentation, and sharing. Discover four iconic dishes that define the local dining table.

Bibimbap

비빔밥

A colorful, nourishing bowl of rice topped with carefully prepared seasoned vegetables, a protein (beef or tofu), a fried egg, and a dollop of spicy-sweet red pepper paste (gochujang).

A perfect balance of textures and tastes. Sautéed savory beef, crunchy vegetables, earthy sesame oil, and a customizable kick of spicy-sweet gochujang paste.

  • Steamed white rice basis
  • Shiitake mushrooms, spinach, bean sprouts, carrots
  • Gochujang (Korean chili paste) & sesame oil
  • Raw or fried egg yolk

Do not eat the toppings individually! Pour a splash of sesame oil, add gochujang paste, and use your spoon to mix everything together vigorously until the egg yolk coats the rice and the color is uniform.

Kimchi

김치

The absolute soul of Korean dining. Kimchi is a fermented side dish made primarily of salted napa cabbage or radishes, seasoned with chili powder, garlic, ginger, and green onions.

Complex, pungent, sour, spicy, and full of umami. The depth of flavor increases as the fermentation matures over weeks or months, creating a sparkling, probiotic-rich crunch.

  • Napa cabbage or Korean radish
  • Gochugaru (coarse red pepper flakes)
  • Garlic, ginger, scallions, salted shrimp/fish sauce
  • Glutinous rice flour paste for seasoning adhesion

Kimchi is almost never eaten as a main meal by itself. It is served as a "banchan" (complimentary side dish) with every meal, paired with warm rice, or used to flavor stews (Kimchi Jjigae) and pancakes (Kimchijeon).

Korean BBQ

고기구이

An interactive, social dining experience where guests grill meats—most notably bulgogi (marinated beef strips) and samgyeopsal (pork belly)—on a charcoal or gas grill built right into the table.

Rich, smoky, and deeply savory. Bulgogi offers a sweet-soy glaze flavor, while samgyeopsal is crispy, fatty, and enhanced by dipping sauces like ssamjang (soy-chili paste).

  • Marinated sirloin (Bulgogi) or thick pork belly (Samgyeopsal)
  • Ssamjang (fermented soybean and chili paste)
  • Lettuce leaves & perilla leaves for wraps
  • Slices of raw garlic, green chili, and green onion salad

Create a "ssam" (wrap)! Take a lettuce leaf, place a piece of grilled meat, add ssamjang paste, garlic, and scallion salad. Fold it into a small parcel and eat it in a single bite—stuffing it in parts is considered messy!

Tteokbokki

떡볶이

Korea's beloved street food comfort. It features chewy, cylinder-shaped white rice cakes cooked in a bright red, sweet and spicy sauce made from gochujang, often mixed with fish cakes and boiled eggs.

Bold, intensely sweet, spicy, and satisfyingly chewy. The thickness of the rice starch creates a heavy, delicious glaze that clings to every element.

  • Garae-tteok (cylindrical thick rice cakes)
  • Eomuk (flat fish cakes)
  • Gochujang chili paste, sugar, and soy sauce glaze
  • Boiled eggs & green scallions

Grab a toothpick or skewer at a street cart ("pojangmacha") and poke a rice cake and fish cake together. It is commonly eaten with fried tempura (튀김) dipped directly in the leftover red tteokbokki sauce.
